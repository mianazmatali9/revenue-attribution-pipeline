Which content actually drives revenue

Content → signup → revenue across four portfolio companies, counted two ways. Every dollar is attributed exactly once per method, so both views reconcile to the same total.

Reconciled to source — $4,011,694.74 under both attribution models

Attributed revenue by channel — last-touch vs first-touch

Last-touch credits the channel a customer came through right before signing up; first-touch credits the channel that first brought them in. They agree on the 71% of customers who took one path, and diverge on the other 29%.

Revenue over time

Revenue retention by channel

Share of a signup cohort still generating revenue N months later. The curve is the same across every channel (~4.4 months avg) — channel drives acquisition volume, not customer value.

The clearest "stop doing" — BrightPath Education

Revenue returned per $1 of spend, by channel. BrightPath's largest line — $65K on YouTube — barely breaks even, while Instagram & Twitter return 30×+ at a fraction of the cost.

How messy is the attribution?

Every user is labelled, none dropped. The dirty slice isn't noise — it's where the two models disagree.

Why you can trust these numbers. The revenue table has ~27,426 rows across 6,170 users (revenue recurs monthly). Attribution enriches each revenue row exactly once, so summing by either method returns $4,011,694.74 — no fan-out, no double-count. An automated test fails the build if attributed revenue ever exceeds total revenue.
What I don't trust: campaign-level ROI (only 10.9% of revenue ties to a specific campaign; the campaign tags are wrong most of the time) · organic is undercounted by design (no tracking tag) · the mid-tier ranking depends on first- vs last-touch · the forecast projects run-off, not a real trajectory.
