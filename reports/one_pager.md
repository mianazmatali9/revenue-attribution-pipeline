# Which content actually drives revenue — and what to do about it

**For:** Portfolio leadership   **From:** Data & Analytics   **Re:** 18 months of content → signup → revenue across the four portfolio companies (**$4.0M** in tracked customer revenue)

---

### Bottom line
Owned content — led by **YouTube and Instagram** — is the real acquisition engine. It drives **~79%** of tracked revenue. "Organic and referral" together are **~21%** — real, but smaller than any one of our major content channels. Marketing is more right than the portfolio CEOs here, with one important nuance about referral.

### The two findings I'd stake my name on

**1. YouTube and Instagram are the top two revenue drivers — and it isn't close for #3.**
Together they account for **$1.31M — about a third (33%) of all tracked revenue.** They rank #1 and #2 whether we credit the *first* thing a customer saw or the *last* thing before they signed up, so this ranking is trustworthy, not an artifact of how we count. YouTube alone is **$672K–$731K** depending on method. *Confidence: high* (large volume, same answer both ways).

**2. Referral punches above its weight — but only at the finish line.**
Referral is credited with **$444K** when we count the *last* touch before signup, but only **$356K** when we count the *first* — the biggest swing of any channel. Translation: word-of-mouth rarely *starts* a customer relationship, but it often *closes* one. It looks small in a "what got them in the door" view and meaningful in a "what sealed the deal" view. *Confidence: medium* (the number depends on which question you're asking).

> *Plain-English note: "last touch" = give 100% of the credit to the last channel the customer came through before signing up. "First touch" = credit the channel that first brought them in. Most customers (71%) took the same channel both times; the other 29% are where the two views disagree.*

### The decision the data implies
- **Put the next marketing dollar into YouTube and Instagram.** They already produce the most revenue, the answer is stable across methods, and where we spend efficiently on them, the return is strong.
- **Stop the oversized YouTube spend on BrightPath Education.** BrightPath put **$65K** into YouTube and earned back about **$1.07 per dollar** — barely breakeven. That same company's Instagram and Twitter returned **$30–$35 per dollar** at a fraction of the cost. Move that budget.
- **Don't cut referral because it "looks small."** It's closing business it doesn't get credit for starting. Protect the partner/referral program; don't fund it like a primary acquisition channel.

### What I don't trust (yet)
- **The middle of the ranking is method-dependent.** Because 29% of customers arrived and converted through *different* channels, which channel is #4 vs #6 (podcast, blog, newsletter, referral) shifts depending on first- vs last-touch. Treat the top two as solid and the middle as a range, not a verdict.
- **Organic is undercounted by design.** Organic sign-ups carry no tracking tag, so its ~10% share is a **floor**, not a precise figure. Its true contribution is probably higher.
- **Campaign-by-campaign ROI is directional only.** I could confidently tie just **11%** of revenue to a specific campaign — the campaign tags in the data point to the wrong channel or company most of the time. I trust *channel*-level conclusions; I do **not** yet trust "Campaign X returned Y×."
- **Ignore the revenue "forecast" trend line.** This sample stops acquiring new customers midway through the window, so recent months are a wind-down of the existing book, not a real decline. A trustworthy forecast needs a live feed where acquisition is ongoing.

### What I'd need to answer the next question
Retention looks **identical across channels** (~4.4 months of revenue on average, whichever channel brought the customer in). So I can't yet say any channel brings *higher-value* customers — only *more* of them. To claim otherwise I'd need clean per-customer lifecycle data with real (not synthetic) churn.
